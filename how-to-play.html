Use this page as a resource to learn the core rules of Dungeons & Dragons, including combat, roleplay, and dice mechanics.

How the Game Works

A typical game moves back and forth between description, decision, and outcome. The DM explains what is happening in the world, the players describe what they want to do, and then the DM decides what happens next. Sometimes an action works automatically. Other times, the result is uncertain, and a die roll is used to determine success or failure.

For example, the DM might describe a locked door at the end of a hallway. One player might want to break it open, another may check for traps, and another might listen for sounds behind it. The players explain their actions, and the DM responds with what happens, sometimes asking for a roll if the outcome is unclear.

What You Need to Start

To begin playing D&D, you generally need a group of players, one Dungeon Master, character sheets, and a set of dice or a digital dice roller. It also helps to have a basic understanding of your own character and a willingness to cooperate, improvise, and have fun. You do not need to know every rule before starting. Most beginners learn by playing, asking questions, and becoming more comfortable over time.

Creating a Character

Your character is the role you play in the story. When creating one, you usually choose a fantasy ancestry, a class, a background, and a personality. These choices determine what your character is good at, how they approach problems, and how they fit into the world.

For a beginner, it helps to keep things simple. Instead of trying to make the most powerful or complicated character possible, focus on making one you understand and enjoy. A good starting point is knowing three things: what your character does well, what they struggle with, and what kind of person they are.

Even a basic concept can be memorable. You might play a brave knight who always puts others first, a curious wizard obsessed with ancient secrets, or a sneaky rogue who acts confident but is secretly nervous all the time. A few clear personality ideas can make roleplaying much easier.

The Combat System

Combat is the more structured part of the game, where timing matters and characters usually take turns acting. It begins when a battle starts or when a dangerous situation needs careful tracking.

At the start of combat, players and enemies determine an order for taking turns. Then each participant acts one at a time until the conflict ends.

On your turn, you can usually move and do one main action, though the exact options depend on your character and the rules being used. Common choices include attacking, casting a spell, helping an ally, using an item, defending yourself, moving into a better position, or trying something creative that the DM allows.

Combat is not just about attacking over and over. Teamwork, positioning, timing, and creativity often matter just as much. Blocking a doorway, protecting a weaker ally, using cover, or interacting with the environment can completely change the outcome of a fight.

Beginner Combat Tips

A helpful habit is to think about your turn before it arrives. Pay attention to what is happening so you are ready when the spotlight reaches you. It also helps to know your character's most common actions ahead of time.

Do not be afraid to ask the DM questions about the battlefield. You can ask where enemies are standing, whether there is cover nearby, what objects are in the room, or whether something looks unstable or dangerous.

Most importantly, remember that combat is a group effort. Characters are usually stronger when they support one another instead of acting completely alone.

Magic and Special Abilities

Some characters rely on magic, while others use weapons, stealth, inventions, training, faith, or pure toughness. As you play, your character will likely gain abilities that make them feel more unique.

If you are playing a character who uses spells, read them carefully. Pay attention to what they do, how far they reach, how long they last, and whether they affect one target, multiple targets, or an area. Beginners often find it useful to keep a short list of favorite or commonly used spells so they are easier to reference during the game.

Even non-magical characters often have special abilities that shape how they play. Learning your own options is much more important than trying to understand every ability in the game at once.

Roleplay

Roleplay is how you decide what your character says, feels, and does. It does not mean you have to perform with a dramatic voice or act like a professional actor. Roleplay can be as simple as making choices the way your character would.

You can speak in first person, such as saying, “I ask the guard to let us pass,” or in third person, such as saying, “My character asks the guard if the group can enter.” Both approaches are completely fine.

A good way to roleplay as a beginner is to think about your character's goals, fears, and personality. Ask yourself simple questions. Are they brave or cautious? Friendly or suspicious? Honest or secretive? Do they trust strangers easily? Do they like danger, or do they avoid it whenever possible?

Exploration

A large part of D&D is exploration. Not every moment is about fighting. Often, the group is traveling, searching rooms, investigating clues, solving puzzles, avoiding traps, or discovering hidden secrets in the world.

When exploring, it helps to ask questions and describe what your character is doing. You might say, “I look around the room for anything unusual,” or “I check the bookshelf for hidden compartments.” The DM will then tell you what you find, and you can decide how to react.

Social Interaction

Talking to people is another major part of D&D. Your group may need to gather rumors in a tavern, negotiate with a village leader, calm an angry spirit, question a witness, or lie your way past a suspicious guard.

These scenes are usually about intent. Be clear about what your character is trying to accomplish. Even if you are not personally confident in social situations, you can still explain your character's goal and let the game mechanics help decide the outcome.

Dice Mechanics

Dice are used when the outcome of an action is uncertain. The most important die in D&D is the twenty-sided die, often called the "d20". You roll it often when trying to do something difficult, risky, or contested.

The Main Dice

  • d20: Used for many important checks, attacks, and saving throws
  • d12, d10, d8, d6, d4: Commonly used for damage, healing, and other effects
  • Percentile dice: Sometimes used for random outcomes or special tables

How Rolling Usually Works

In many situations, you roll a d20 and add a number from your character sheet. The total is compared to a target set by the rules or the DM. If your total is high enough, you succeed. If not, you fail or only partially succeed, depending on the situation.

The DM may ask for a roll when you attempt something like sneaking past a guard, convincing a merchant to lower a price, climbing a crumbling wall, resisting a magical effect, or striking an enemy during battle.

Advantage and Disadvantage

Sometimes the game makes a roll easier or harder. When you have advantage, you roll two d20s and use the higher result. When you have disadvantage, you roll two d20s and use the lower result.

This is one of the simplest and most common ways the game reflects favorable or unfavorable conditions. For example, hiding in darkness might give you an edge, while trying to shoot through heavy fog might make things harder.
